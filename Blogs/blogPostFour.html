Blog Post #4:

Dissecting Model Visuals


Webpages are reproductions of early forms of gathering, organising, and distributing data such as literature, news media, and other forms of print media. It is for this reason that website are synonymously referred to web pages. Therefore, it is no surprise that for websites to effectively perform their intended work, they’ve borrowed conventions from print media. Particularly with regards to the way that users scan or read or visualise webpages.


According to (Knafflic 2015), a user’s eyes are drawn first to the top left of a page. Therefore, it is important to consider this to be able to establish certain strategies that will determine what is emphasised or de-emphasised on the page. As developers we are often on a mission to create for the user (user-centric design) and in doing so developers often create a visual hierarchy in which certain elements within the page are allocated a specific level of priority represented through the use of colour, scale, and proportion. This is important because a user’s priority is always ease of access and by establishing a visual hierarchy that they can follow removes any clutter that might obstruct their user experience of a webpage.


Therefore, it is important to consider how the use or otherwise, affect the user experience of a page because establishing a visual hierarchy determines how the information architecture of the site is perceived and understood, and at the end of the day engages users because they can attribute their attention to a specific focal point on the page. This will be very useful in considering how I can make my data set easy to understand and fun to consume because we make data visualisations to intrigue them in to getting to know about the data, but how we arrange and choose to design our visual hierarchy on the graphs we can not only intrigue users with visuals but engage with them.


References:


Knafflic, C. (2015). Storytelling with data. Wiley. (6) 151 – 163.
