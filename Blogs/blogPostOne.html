Blog Post 1:

What is Data Visualisation?


Imagine a universal library filled with every book that is and ‘not’ in existence, deemed into existence by discovery. This is what the internet personifies. And with all that there is to discover on the internet data visualisation has significantly localised invention and discovery throughout the internet.

The development of the internet and computer software as Li (2020) rightly points out, “has advanced the application of data visualisation, allowing users to manipulate a substantial amount of data for exploration and analysis more easily and affordably”. This means that the aim of using images to gain insights and knowledge from discovering data and its patterns and relationships can be achieved. This is extremely important because the far-reaching impact of the science behind how data is collected, linked, and created on the internet necessitates pictorial or graphical interfacing to present, contextualise, and give data meaning.

How is this reflected in my site?


Well this is personified through the types of graphs that are present within the site, because these graphs use shapes and text to communicate the information outlayed by the data. However, some of the graphs present in the website provide the user with buttons to manipulate and change the data set that they wish to examine.


An example of data visualised are infographics. According to the Oxford English Dictionary, an infographic is “a visual representation of information or data”. Infographics help audiences understand and remember the content of the infographic. My first encounter with infographics was on pamphlets, flyers, and brochures. And from this, it is clear that infographics are meant to readily provide an easily readable, and quick rundown of something that would be difficult to explain in the moment from a promoter (because you are in a rush to go to work) for example.


What is my Opinion on them?


I find infographics particularly effective in conveying a significant amount of information in a very effective way. Their use of text and visuals often sometimes remind me about comics, and how the use of these two mediums encapsulate a multimodal motif that contextualises the reasons behind and the inforgraphic through its visuals and reinforces the meaning and message through text - much like comics do.


Does this make sense?


Yes, Infographics often contain text, images to represent their intent, but data visualisation in its traditional form is represented with trees, charts, diagrams, and graphs. These are common ways of visualising data that is largely understood to co-relate, differentiate, and compare data. Because ultimately what data visualisation seeks to do is build perception, invite opinion, and cultivate knowledge. In conclusion, the lesson that can be learned from this article is that data visualisation is a need. Because humans navigate lives through meaningful means of forecasting events based on co-relating, differentiating, and comparing data as means to understand the context we live in. Therefore, through this newfound understanding of how information can be organised and subsequently interpreted it is apparent that data visualisation creates and enables storytelling and narrative.
