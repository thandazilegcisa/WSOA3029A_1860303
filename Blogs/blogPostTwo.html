Blog Post #2:

The structure, architecture, or layout of a website is no trivial practice. It is important because it is how information is accessed and sequenced. Hence, the structure and layout of a website serve to convey the information architecture of a website. To display the possibilities of navigating through an application. And in the case of Twitch – our chosen case study, its user interface and user experience speak to exactly that. Mainly because Twitch understands the context of its medium, and thus follows several conventions that have partly framed how the layout and structure of its website are understood to be.


Considering the real estate of a website is very important. And in the image, above we see that although a lot of information is presented to the user the scale of how images and text is presented follows a hierarchy. The logo in the top left corner establishes or reinforces the colour palette of the website and is placed at this position to meet user’s needs because users have already adopted presumptions and conventions about how information is presented on the web, thus much of the design of Twitch is user-centric. I say this because, for one, the search bar calls the user to action to search/browse for channels that they could watch to convert the user into browsing the architecture of the app. And this visual motif or hierarchy is maintained through the site because functions that aid the discovery of channels is prioritised, even though just the font-weight. For example, the names, and messages of users use a different font size compared to the streamers.


This is mainly because the streamers represent Twitch, they contextualise the app as what it is – a video streaming site, which gives meaning to the platform. This is also the reason why the video screen is the main focal point of the site. Additionally, Twitch has very interesting user experience features, including The Hype Train. This is when users of the app subscribe to a streamers channel, and when multiple users accumulate subscriptions over a short space of time it creates a hype train. This is interesting because it invites users to support the streamers and subsequently the platform. Though this is not the only way this goal is achieved. It is also achieved by creating a badge or leader board for a user who gifts the most subscriptions or emotes that streamers curate to use in their channel.


Though this might be a bias interpretation of what the colour red means within the website, the colour red is easily readable and draws attention because it carries weight, and as such it is used to bring attention to important notifications and stats for users such as the number of viewers watching, and if a broadcast is live or not. So, what can be learnt from this modest analysis is that conventions do most of the work for the developer especially with regards to usability and accessibility because of an established understanding of how user interfaces are structured on the web. And that the visual hierarchy of how information is subsequently as the structure itself because it seeks to interpret the structure by giving it colour, size, density, and value.
