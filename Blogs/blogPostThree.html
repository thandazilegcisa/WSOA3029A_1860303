Blog Post #3:

This article examines the history of data visualisation and the history it foregrounds through pictures, dates, numeric and textual descriptions. According to Friendly (2008) “before the 17 century, data visualisations existed mainly in the realm of maps, displaying land marker, cities, roads, and resources”. However, as populations grew, the industrial revolution took place during the 19 century.


The need to organise and plan for economies, medicine, commerce, transportation, and the military took priority. This gave rise to what has come to be known as the golden age of statistical graphing. And with the use of graphical displays for understanding complex data established, many new graphical forms of interpreting data were invented and extended into new areas of inquiry including the Rose Diagram by Florence Nightingale, which is the object of our study. Data, often than not, is contextual. And because data is often collected from a primary source, considering what the data seeks to represent is extremely important. By looking at what it seeks to represent we decode the various means of communication it signifies, be it text, pictures, or numbers.


We do this by first establishing the context or discourse, and then establish the difference we seek within this context, which is commonly done by stacking figures on a bar graph, or in Florence Nightingale’s case stacking proportions on a rose diagram. In a 2016 article Clive Thompson cites that Nightingale’s visualisation was foregrounded by war, a war in which she deemed not to be the cause of high mortality rates but rather unsanitary conditions for militants. Hence, her rose diagram compared the number of people dying from war, diseases, and other causes, and her findings found that more people were dying from causes unrelated to war such as disease. Her stark revelations and visualisations exalted her to become the first person to successfully use data visualisation to persuade and influence public policy. Because public servants quickly amended policy to provide healthily, and sanitary conditions to honour militant’s work. This visualisation was great because it produced form, shape, and colour to separate ideas, which rendered the data collected easily readable, and indeed usable.


This form of visualisation though not quite popular in business, economics, and government departments. Has taken the form of a loading bar that certain applications use to indicate the collection or buffering of a website or app. Therefore, the main takeaway from this article is that data visualisation provides a frame of reference (context) within which audiences can interpret and differentiate information easily and clearly to persuade audiences to either change policy, become aware of their biases, or promote consumption.
